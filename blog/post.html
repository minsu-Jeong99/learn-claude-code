Claude 3.7 Sonnet: Extended Thinking and the Frontier of Reasoning

Our most capable model introduces a new paradigm for complex problem-solving — one that mirrors how experts approach difficult questions by thinking through them carefully, revising assumptions, and exploring multiple paths before settling on an answer.

Language models have, until recently, operated under a quiet assumption: that each token generated is the final word on the matter. Autoregressive generation is inherently committed — each step forecloses alternatives, building a narrative that is difficult to revise without starting over. For conversational tasks, this works beautifully. For genuinely hard problems, it introduces a ceiling.

Claude 3.7 Sonnet challenges that ceiling. With the introduction of Extended Thinking, the model now has access to a private reasoning scratchpad — a space to work through difficult problems, question its own assumptions, and explore competing lines of argument before committing to a response. The result is a model that doesn't just generate text; it deliberates.

What is Extended Thinking?

Extended Thinking gives Claude the ability to produce a stream of internal reasoning — visible to the developer but separate from the final response — before arriving at its answer. Think of it as the difference between a student who blurts out the first thing that comes to mind and one who works through the problem on a scratchpad before writing the final answer on the exam.

The reasoning tokens are not filler. They are the actual cognitive work — the place where Claude reconsiders, contradicts itself, explores dead ends, and changes course.

— From our internal model evaluation report

This distinction matters enormously. Previous approaches to "chain of thought" prompting asked models to reason in the same output channel as their answer, which creates subtle pressure toward confident-sounding reasoning rather than genuinely exploratory thought. Extended Thinking decouples the two, giving Claude permission to be uncertain in the scratchpad while being precise in the response.

How It Works

At the architecture level, Extended Thinking introduces a two-phase generation process. In the first phase, Claude generates reasoning tokens using a modified sampling strategy that encourages exploration — higher temperature, reduced repetition penalties, and explicit training to revise prior reasoning tokens. In the second phase, Claude generates the final response conditioned on both the original input and the full reasoning trace.

The Thinking Budget

One of the most practically important aspects of Extended Thinking is the configurable "thinking budget" — the maximum number of tokens Claude is permitted to spend on internal reasoning before producing a response. This allows developers to make an explicit tradeoff between latency, cost, and reasoning quality:

Python 
import anthropic

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-3-7-sonnet-20250219",
    max_tokens=16000,
    thinking={
        "type": "enabled",
        "budget_tokens": 10000  # How much to think before responding
    },
    messages=[{
        "role": "user",
        "content": "Prove that the square root of 2 is irrational."
    }]
)

# Reasoning trace — for debugging and analysis
for block in response.content:
    if block.type == "thinking":
        print(f"Reasoning: {block.thinking}")
    elif block.type == "text":
        print(f"Response: {block.text}")

The thinking budget acts as a ceiling, not a target. Claude will use as many thinking tokens as it judges necessary — often far fewer than the maximum — and then produce its response. Setting a larger budget primarily increases the upper bound of problem complexity the model can address, not the average latency of every request.

Tip for developers

For most production use cases, a budget of 5,000–8,000 tokens strikes a good balance. Reserve larger budgets (10,000+) for problems that genuinely require deep analysis: complex proofs, multi-step reasoning chains, or nuanced judgment calls with many competing considerations.

Benchmark Results

The performance improvements from Extended Thinking are most pronounced in domains that require multi-step reasoning, planning, and self-correction. On the GPQA Diamond benchmark — a set of graduate-level science questions designed to resist pattern-matching and require genuine understanding — Claude 3.7 Sonnet achieves 84.8% accuracy with Extended Thinking enabled, compared to 74.3% without it.

On mathematical competition problems (AIME 2024), the model solves 62.5% of problems with Extended Thinking, approaching the performance threshold of highly trained human competitors. This represents a 28-point improvement over Claude 3.5 Sonnet on the same benchmark.

In software engineering tasks (SWE-bench Verified), the model achieves 70.3% on the full test set — meaning it successfully addresses roughly 7 in 10 real-world GitHub issues when given the ability to reason through the problem before writing code.

Using the API

Extended Thinking is available today through the Anthropic API to all developers. The feature is opt-in — existing integrations are not affected. When thinking is enabled, the response object includes both thinking blocks (the internal reasoning trace) and text blocks (the final response).

A few important notes for developers building with Extended Thinking:

  • Thinking tokens count toward your output token usage and are billed accordingly.
  • The reasoning trace is not sent back to users by default — it is provided to the developer for debugging and analysis.
  • Streaming is fully supported for both thinking and response blocks.
  • Extended Thinking is compatible with tool use, system prompts, and all existing API features.

Safety Considerations

Giving a model access to private reasoning raises important questions about interpretability and alignment. If Claude can think things it doesn't say, how do we ensure that its reasoning is honest and aligned with its stated values?

We've approached this through a combination of training interventions and ongoing monitoring. Claude is trained to reason in ways that are genuinely exploratory rather than deceptive — the goal of the scratchpad is to improve quality, not to obscure intent. Our safety team reviews samples of thinking traces as part of our ongoing model evaluation process.

Transparency about model internals is not optional. As capabilities increase, the ability to inspect and understand how a model reaches its conclusions becomes more important, not less.

We will publish a more detailed technical report on the safety properties of Extended Thinking in the coming weeks, including results from our interpretability research team's analysis of the reasoning traces.

What's Next

Extended Thinking in Claude 3.7 Sonnet is the first step toward a broader research program we call "deliberative alignment" — the idea that safe, helpful AI requires not just good values but the ability to reason carefully about how to apply those values in complex, novel situations. We believe that giving models genuine reasoning capacity is not in tension with safety; it is, ultimately, a prerequisite for it.

Over the coming months, we plan to release more detailed analysis of how Extended Thinking affects performance across domains, additional tools for developers to analyze and visualize reasoning traces, and research on how reasoning quality scales with model size and training compute.

Claude 3.7 Sonnet with Extended Thinking is available now through the Anthropic API and Claude.ai for Pro and Team subscribers.
